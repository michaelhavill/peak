## Teardown 1: Product Designer - Homepage Hero Copy

**Prompt:** "Write three hero headline options for the homepage of our B2B onboarding tool."

**Before (generic prompt, no taste doc):**
> 1. "Streamline your onboarding with our intuitive, AI-powered platform."
> 2. "Get your team productive faster with seamless onboarding solutions."
> 3. "Transform employee experience with our all-in-one onboarding suite."

**Taste doc loaded** (3 specific rules): *"Hero copy must name the user's current pain in their own words. Never use the words 'streamline', 'seamless', 'solution', or 'platform'. Lead with the outcome, not the capability."*

**After (same prompt, taste doc in context):**
> 1. "Your new hire is still waiting for IT access on day four. Fix that."
> 2. "Stop losing the first two weeks of every new hire to setup."
> 3. "New hires shipping real work in week one, not week five."

**Read the delta.** The generic set is interchangeable with every other B2B SaaS homepage in history. The taste-loaded set names the pain in the user's own language, refuses the corporate nouns, and leads with outcome. Same model. Same prompt. Three rules in the middle changed everything.

**What changed downstream.** The designer stopped reviewing hero copy in rounds of three. The first draft landed. Six weeks of review cycles disappeared. Her time went back to the IA work that actually required her judgment.

## Teardown 2: Creative Director - Campaign Concept Brief

**Prompt:** "Draft three campaign concepts for a premium consumer electronics launch."

**Before (generic prompt):**
> Concept 1: "Unleash your potential" - hero imagery of diverse users excelling at their passions, lifestyle-driven shots, aspirational copy.
> Concept 2: "The future, refined" - clean product-focused shots against minimal backgrounds, emphasis on craftsmanship and design.
> Concept 3: "Designed for you" - personalization theme, customers in their environments, emotional resonance.

**Taste doc loaded** (5 specific rules): *"Earn attention in 3 seconds or the concept is dead. The tension must be visible without a caption. Premium visual vocabulary, never stock. Every concept lands an unspoken truth about the category, never celebrates the product. If a competitor could run the same concept, kill it."*

**After (same prompt, taste doc in context):**
> Concept 1: "The Last Thing You Buy." Visual: a cluttered desk of past-generation devices in greyscale, one colour device in pristine focus. No person. No lifestyle. The category truth: you've been sold the next thing for ten years running.
> Concept 2: "It Doesn't Notice You." Visual: the device functioning flawlessly in the ambient periphery of a complex life. No hero shot. The category truth: premium tech earns its place by disappearing, not demanding attention.
> Concept 3: "Keep Your Receipts." Visual: the device alongside a handwritten spec sheet of everything its predecessors couldn't do. Almost anti-glossy. The category truth: every premium launch promises; almost none deliver.

**Read the delta.** The generic set is every premium tech launch since 2012. The taste-loaded set refuses the product-celebration posture, lands a category truth in each concept, and rejects the hero-shot convention entirely. Same model. Same prompt. Five rules rejected the default aesthetic.

**What changed downstream.** The agency's concept-to-approval cycle compressed from 9 days to 2. Clients started asking why the work felt so consistent across different creatives. Answer: every creative was starting from the same floor - the floor the taste doc enforced.

## Teardown 3: Marketing Lead - Email Subject Lines

**Prompt:** "Give me 10 subject lines for our weekly customer newsletter."

**Before (generic prompt):**
> 1. "This week's top insights from [Company]"
> 2. "Your weekly round-up is here"
> 3. "5 things you need to know this week"
> 4. "Don't miss this week's updates"
> 5. "Your [Company] weekly digest"
> (...five more, all tonally identical)

**Taste doc loaded** (4 specific rules): *"Never use the word 'weekly', 'digest', 'round-up', 'insights', or 'don't miss'. Lead with a claim, a number, or a question - never a description of the email. Tuesday sends land best for our audience. Mobile-opened in a 40-character window."*

**After (same prompt, taste doc in context):**
> 1. "Why Shopify killed their headcount plan in Q1"
> 2. "The $840K per year most teams don't realise they're burning"
> 3. "Three pricing mistakes we see every founder make"
> 4. "What does your AI know about you?"
> 5. "The retention pattern no one's tracking"
> (...five more, all leading with claim or question, all under 40 chars)

**Read the delta.** The generic set describes the email. The taste-loaded set sells the open. The length constraint keeps them mobile-first. The banned-words rule alone eliminates the entire SaaS-newsletter aesthetic.

**What changed downstream.** Open rates climbed from 19% to 42%. The lead no longer needed to rewrite every subject line before shipping. That's two reclaimed hours per week redirected to the deep content work that actually compounds.

## Teardown 4: Product Designer - UX Flow Decision

**Prompt:** "Recommend an onboarding flow structure for a new B2B financial tool."

**Before (generic prompt):**
> A 7-step onboarding covering: (1) account creation, (2) team setup, (3) integrations, (4) permissions configuration, (5) workflow selection, (6) notification preferences, (7) welcome tour.

**Taste doc loaded** (4 specific rules): *"Progressive disclosure on complex flows - never more than 3 decisions per screen. Information architecture follows the user's mental model, not the org chart. Time-to-value in onboarding is the primary metric; feature completeness is a trap. Admin decisions belong in a secondary setup flow, never in first-run."*

**After (same prompt, taste doc in context):**
> A 3-step first-run: (1) What's the one thing you want to do in the next 10 minutes? (2) Here's the sample data you need. (3) Here's the result. Every admin decision (team setup, permissions, integrations, notifications) moves into a "Setup later" sidebar that the user enters by choice, not by default.

**Read the delta.** The generic flow is an admin checklist. The taste-loaded flow is a product. The four rules forced a rejection of the default "show them everything" posture and reframed the entire structure around time-to-value.

**What changed downstream.** The designer stopped spending IA cycles rejecting the default 7-step pattern. Her team's AI now drafts flows that respect progressive disclosure on the first pass. Every subsequent review conversation moves from "why is this 7 steps?" to "is the right value in step 3?"

## Teardown 5: Creative Director - Brand Voice Edit

**Prompt:** "Rewrite this product announcement to match our voice: *'We're excited to announce the launch of our new premium subscription tier, designed to offer power users enhanced features and capabilities.'*"

**Before (generic prompt):**
> "We're thrilled to introduce our new premium subscription - built for power users who want more control, more features, and a deeper experience. Upgrade today to unlock the full potential of our platform."

**Taste doc loaded** (6 specific rules): *"Never use the words 'excited', 'thrilled', 'delighted', 'proud', 'unlock', 'potential', 'journey'. No opening self-congratulation. Write as if the reader is smarter than you and short on time. Every sentence earns its place or it cuts. Specific over superlative."*

**After (same prompt, taste doc in context):**
> "The subscription tier is out. It does four things the standard version doesn't: higher API limits, team seats, SSO, and priority response times. Here's who it's for and who it isn't."

**Read the delta.** The generic rewrite lands exactly where 10,000 other product announcements land. The taste-loaded rewrite strips the performative vocabulary, names the specific capabilities, and refuses the self-congratulatory opener. Six rules killed six defaults.

**What changed downstream.** The founder stopped being the last human in the chain on announcements. Junior marketers' first drafts landed. A hard-won voice - the thing that took five years to develop - became a renewable asset instead of a bottleneck.

## Teardown 6: Marketing Lead - Case Study Draft

**Prompt:** "Draft a customer case study based on this transcript of an interview with a Series B fintech CTO."

**Before (generic prompt):**
> "When [Customer]'s engineering team needed to scale their platform to meet growing demand, they turned to [Company]. By implementing our solution, they were able to streamline their operations, reduce costs, and empower their developers. 'We're thrilled with the results,' says the CTO. 'The team has never been more productive.'"

**Taste doc loaded** (5 specific rules): *"Case studies lead with the problem specificity, never the customer logo. Name the dollar cost of the old state. Name the dollar value of the new state. Real quotes only; never paraphrase toward the sales narrative. Never say 'streamline', 'empower', 'scale', or 'solution'."*

**After (same prompt, taste doc in context):**
> "Their deploy pipeline was taking 42 minutes per release. At eight releases a week, that's 90 engineering hours per month - roughly $18K of senior IC time - spent waiting on CI. They re-architected their pipeline using our orchestration layer and brought it to 6 minutes. The recovered hours redirected into a backlog of platform reliability work they'd postponed for two quarters. 'We didn't replace our pipeline. We stopped paying the pipeline tax,' the CTO said in the interview."

**Read the delta.** The generic version is a sales sheet with a quote grafted on. The taste-loaded version is a case study - specific numbers, specific problem, real quote. Five rules rejected the entire genre's default posture.

**What changed downstream.** The marketing lead stopped being the editor who turned generic case studies into usable ones. The first drafts shipped. The sales team started using case studies that landed because they were specific, not performative. Win rate on late-stage deals went up 15%.

## The Pattern Across All Six

Every teardown shows the same three things.

**One: the rules are small.** Three to six specific rules per domain. Not "write clearly." Not "we value quality." *"Never use the word 'seamless'. Body text never exceeds 18px. Lead with a customer pain, not a TAM slide."*

**Two: the deltas are large.** First-draft output goes from "needs a full rewrite" to "ship it with light edits." The 45-minute rewrite tax compresses to five minutes of judgment.

**Three: the economics compound.** Every rule you add closes a recurring leak. Every case you review teaches a new rule. Every rule improves every subsequent output. The taste doc becomes a living balance-sheet item that appreciates with use.

## Run Your Own Teardown This Week

Pick one task you do repeatedly. Run it twice: once with a blank prompt, once with a five-rule taste doc in context. Paste both outputs into a doc side by side. Read the delta yourself. If it doesn't stop you cold, your rules aren't specific enough. Write sharper rules and run it again.

That is the entire onramp. One task, two runs, one afternoon. The compounding starts the moment you close the loop.
