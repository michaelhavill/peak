There are two versions of the same career happening right now. One is compounding through AI into something irreplaceable. The other is using AI to do the same work faster - and quietly commoditizing every month. Both versions feel productive week to week. They diverge dramatically over 18 months.

This piece is a 10-marker rubric to score yourself honestly against. The markers separate the operators who are elevating from the ones who are merely automating. The diagnosis tells you which trajectory you're actually on. The fix is specific and actionable.

Featured archetypes: **CEO**, **VP Engineering**, **Head of Product**.

## How to Score

Ten markers. For each, give yourself 0-5:

- **0** - the opposite is true
- **2** - you aspire to it; you don't practise it
- **3** - sometimes true
- **4** - broadly true; small gaps
- **5** - fully embedded in how you work

Total 0-50. Interpretation at the end.

---

## The Ten Markers

### 1. Your promotion signals are shipped outcomes, not hours

**The marker.** Your last promotion, and the one before it, tied directly to operational outcomes - metrics moved, revenue driven, risk retired - not to time invested or artefacts produced.

**Why it matters.** Commoditized careers measure on activity. Irreplaceable careers measure on impact. If the former still governs your comp conversations, you're on the wrong trajectory regardless of how much AI you use.

### 2. AI-assisted work is your baseline, not your edge

**The marker.** You assume AI involvement in every professional output. You would be embarrassed to ship work without it. The question isn't "did you use AI?" - it's "what judgment did you apply on top of it?"

**Why it matters.** AI-as-edge is last year's story. AI-as-baseline is where the market is pricing now. People still positioning "I use AI well" as a differentiator are running a depreciating asset.

### 3. Your calendar shifted from execution to direction

**The marker.** Six months ago you spent most of your time executing work. Today you spend most of it directing AI workflows, reviewing outputs, and deciding which bets to take. Your execution ratio dropped. Your judgment ratio rose.

**Why it matters.** The commoditized version of any role is the one where a person still does the assembly by hand. The irreplaceable version is the one where a person applies judgment to assembly AI did for them.

### 4. You ship things you could not have shipped alone

**The marker.** Your recent output includes work you physically could not have produced without AI orchestration - more scope, more parallel streams, deeper synthesis across domains than a single human can hold in working memory.

**Why it matters.** Elevation is doing work that was previously impossible. Automation is doing old work faster. Only one moves you up the stack.

### 5. Your judgment is encoded, not just exercised

**The marker.** Some portion of your expertise lives outside your head - in a knowledge base, in taste docs, in a schema file for your AI. You review and refine that encoded judgment regularly. It is deployed alongside your real-time thinking.

**Why it matters.** Exercised expertise lives with you one meeting at a time. Encoded expertise compounds daily. The former cannot be levered. The latter is the asset.

### 6. Your decisions draw from a wider aperture than your own experience

**The marker.** Your recent high-stakes calls were informed by disciplines outside your own - pricing psychology, regulatory precedent, adjacent-industry failure patterns, cross-functional context. You consulted them because AI made consultation cheap.

**Why it matters.** A career shaped by only your own experience has a narrowing ceiling. A career shaped by every relevant discipline in every decision has a widening one.

### 7. You redirected reclaimed time to strategic work

**The marker.** The hours AI gave back didn't disappear into more volume. They went into deep thinking, strategic bets, creative craft, and problems that were previously deferred because nothing had time for them.

**Why it matters.** This is the move that separates compounding careers from running-faster careers. Same hours reclaimed. Totally different destination.

### 8. You can articulate your specific moat

**The marker.** In one sentence you can state the specific combination of judgment, context, and craft that makes your work inimitable - and you can point to the artefacts where it's encoded.

**Why it matters.** If you can't articulate it, you don't have one. Generic skill + generic AI = generic output. The moat only forms where specificity lives.

### 9. Your performance reviews shifted format

**The marker.** Your last performance review measured on output, impact, and strategic contribution - not on process compliance or activity metrics. AI-usage patterns were reviewed as telemetry, not celebrated as virtue.

**Why it matters.** The companies that still reward activity over impact will commoditize their top performers into middling ones. The companies that shifted the measurement frame keep them.

### 10. Your work is compounding, not depreciating

**The marker.** The work you shipped this quarter made next quarter's work easier - through knowledge-base deposits, refined taste rules, better-tuned workflows. If you stopped today, your past work would still be paying dividends for at least the next year.

**Why it matters.** Commoditized work evaporates the moment you stop. Compounding work keeps earning for years after. The trajectory is decided by which kind you're running.

| **16-30** | Mixed trajectory. You are getting marginal gains from AI but have not restructured your career around elevation. | **41-50** | Rare. You are the operator your peers will study in 2-3 years. Your challenge is not sliding back - markers 1, 7, and 9 erode first as orgs change around you. Protect them. |

## The Three Moves That Fix the Most

If you scored below 30, three interventions return the highest ROI.

**One: encode your judgment this quarter.** Not someday. This quarter. Pick your strongest domain and write the 15 rules you would give a sharp new hire on day one. Connect them to your AI. Move from exercised to encoded expertise. This is marker 5 and it unlocks markers 3, 6, 8, and 10 downstream.

**Two: redirect reclaimed time intentionally.** Every week, track the hours AI gave back. Put them on your calendar for strategic work - customer conversations, deep thinking, hard bets - before they disappear into more volume. This is marker 7 and it is the only discipline that keeps the elevation play from quietly collapsing back into automation.

**Three: articulate the moat in one sentence.** If you can't write it down, you don't have one. Write a sentence that names the specific judgment, specific context, and specific craft that makes your work inimitable. Then audit your last quarter against it. Are you actually deploying that combination, or are you performing it? The answer shapes everything downstream.

---

## Run It Quarterly

This rubric isn't a one-shot assessment. It's a quarterly ritual. The trajectories are slow-moving but decisive. Scoring yourself every 90 days surfaces the drift before it becomes a trap. The operators I've watched do this consistently - **CEOs**, **VPs of Engineering**, **Heads of Product** - are the ones who stay on the elevation curve through multiple technology waves, not just this one.

Run the rubric. Fix the bottom three. Re-run in 90 days. The teams that make this a habit separate from the ones that don't within two cycles. Full stop.
