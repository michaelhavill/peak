Every AI rollout I've watched has hit the same wall: adoption stalls at 20%. Not because the tools are bad. Because the culture rewards the wrong behaviour. Doers ship with AI. Talkers present about AI. The team that hires and promotes one will pull away from the team that rewards the other every single quarter. This piece is a 12-item rubric to score your own culture against. The scores tell you exactly where your AI investment is leaking.

Featured archetypes: **founder**, **head of people**, **recruiter**.

## How to Score

Twelve markers below. For each, give yourself a score from 0 to 5.

- **0** - the opposite is true in your culture
- **2** - you talk about it; nobody practises it
- **3** - some teams practise it; most don't
- **4** - broadly practised; a few holdouts
- **5** - embedded, rewarded, reinforced

Total score 0-60. Interpretation at the bottom. No grading on a curve. No "we're different, it doesn't apply." Run the rubric honestly or don't run it.

---

## The Twelve Markers

### 1. Ship-first hiring

**The marker.** Your interview loop rewards candidates who show up with a shipped portfolio of AI-assisted work, not candidates who present a tidy deck about their AI philosophy.

**What 5/5 looks like.** Every hiring panel includes a live exercise - "here's a real problem from our backlog; show me how you'd use AI to start on it now." Candidates who can only describe process fail out in round one.

### 2. Promotion signals output, not activity

**The marker.** Promotions at your company are visibly tied to things shipped, metrics moved, and operational impact - not to hours logged, meetings attended, or artefacts produced that nobody downstream used.

**What 5/5 looks like.** Every promotion packet leads with a shipped-outcomes section. Cases without concrete output don't get through.

### 3. Knowledge is shared infrastructure, not personal leverage

**The marker.** Senior people in your company actively deposit their expertise into the shared knowledge base. They are rewarded for doing so. They do not hoard context for political protection.

**What 5/5 looks like.** Contribution to the knowledge base is a named expectation at senior levels. Annual reviews reference it. Compounding the team's collective expertise is treated as a first-class responsibility.

### 4. AI fluency is a baseline expectation

**The marker.** Every role description includes AI-native workflow expectations. Hiring filters screen for it. Onboarding assumes it. Nobody gets "a pass because they're new."

**What 5/5 looks like.** Week-one onboarding includes hands-on AI work against a real backlog item. By end of week one, new hires have shipped something AI-assisted with a senior reviewer.

### 5. The "I explored options" behaviour is flagged

**The marker.** Your culture notices when someone is performing exploration instead of shipping. Leadership actively redirects talkers toward doing rather than validating their process theatre.

**What 5/5 looks like.** In stand-ups and retros, "I explored X" without "and here's what I shipped from it" gets a direct, respectful push-back, not a nod.

### 6. AI adoption has a curve, not a flat line

**The marker.** Your team's AI adoption is measurably increasing quarter over quarter. People who weren't using it six months ago are using it now. The 20% ceiling does not exist in your org because you actively broke through it.

**What 5/5 looks like.** A named owner tracks AI usage per team, reports it in leadership reviews, and runs explicit interventions when a team stalls.

### 7. One-on-one tutoring beats webinars

**The marker.** Your AI training is structured as senior-to-individual tutoring, not mass webinars. The person who needs it most gets 30 minutes with a doer, not a 90-minute recorded session they fast-forward through.

**What 5/5 looks like.** Every team has a named "AI peer" who runs weekly 1:1s with slow-adopter teammates for the first quarter of rollout.

### 8. Output artefacts reveal AI involvement

**The marker.** People on your team show their work. PRs reference AI-assisted drafts. Briefs cite AI-generated synthesis. Slides note AI-assembled data. The use of AI is normalised, not hidden.

**What 5/5 looks like.** The team culture treats disclosing AI use as normal engineering practice, the same way you'd cite a library or a research source.

### 9. The worst performers were also the worst AI users

**The marker.** When you audit performance, the bottom quartile of your team overlaps heavily with the bottom quartile of AI adoption. You have real data showing the correlation.

**What 5/5 looks like.** Managers review AI-usage telemetry alongside output metrics during perf cycles. The correlation is surfaced, not buried.

### 10. The best performers' AI workflows are studied

**The marker.** Your top performers' AI workflows are documented, shared, and replicated. What they're doing becomes the team standard, not a personal advantage.

**What 5/5 looks like.** A "how I work with AI" deposit from top performers lives in the shared knowledge base. New hires read it in week one.

### 11. Meeting-to-output ratio is declining

**The marker.** Your team holds fewer meetings per shipped unit of work each quarter. Context lives in the knowledge base and AI-assembled pre-reads, so meetings shift from catching up to making decisions.

**What 5/5 looks like.** Standing meetings get cancelled regularly because the context they used to enforce is now in the knowledge base. Nobody misses them.

### 12. The talker-to-doer hiring ratio is tracked

**The marker.** Someone on your team keeps an informal record of whether recent hires turned out to be doers or talkers. That record informs future sourcing, interviewing, and leveling decisions.

**What 5/5 looks like.** The **head of people** or the **founder** can tell you, by name, which of the last 10 hires are in each bucket - and what the pattern in the misses taught you.

## Your Total

| Score | Diagnosis |
|-------|-----------|
| **0-20** | Your AI investment is being extracted at roughly 10-20% of its potential. Your culture is rewarding talk, not shipping. The 20% adoption wall isn't a bug - it's the predictable output of how you hire, promote, and evaluate. Intervention needed before more budget is deployed. |
| **21-35** | Mixed culture. Some teams ship; others perform. You're extracting ~40-60% of your AI investment. The fix is identifying the 2-3 markers where you're weakest and making them explicit, named expectations with named owners. |
| **36-50** | Strong doer culture. You're extracting most of the available value. Your remaining gaps are usually around the "knowledge as shared infrastructure" and "tracking the talker-to-doer ratio" markers - the discipline moves that separate a good team from an enduring moat. |
| **51-60** | Rare. You are the team competitors study. Your challenge is staying here as you scale - every 10 new hires dilutes the culture unless markers 1, 4, and 12 stay sharp. |

---

## The Three Moves That Fix the Most

If you scored below 35, three interventions return the highest ROI.

**One: rewrite your job descriptions.** Add AI-native workflow expectations to every role. Filter for shipped-evidence at the source. Stop interviewing talkers that ship nothing. This is the cheapest fix and the highest-leverage.

**Two: run AI-usage one-on-ones for a quarter.** For every teammate below the median on adoption, a named senior peer runs a weekly 30-minute session with real work. Adoption moves from 20% to 70%+ within the quarter in every team I've watched try this.

**Three: make the knowledge base a reviewable expectation.** Senior contributions to the knowledge base become a named expectation in role descriptions and annual reviews. Your institutional memory stops walking out the door. Your team's AI stops starting from zero.

Run the rubric. Score honestly. Fix the three weakest markers first. Re-run in 90 days. The teams that make this a quarterly ritual separate from the teams that don't within two cycles.
