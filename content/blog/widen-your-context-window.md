> **Foundations assumed:** [Knowledge bases are the new career capital](/blog/pillar-1-knowledge-management) · [Encode your specificity](/blog/you-are-not-generic)

You are making every high-stakes decision through a keyhole. The keyhole is shaped like your own experience - and until now, that was the best lens available. AI just invented a panoramic window. The operators who already opened it are running a different game. The ones still looking through the keyhole keep losing to them and can't figure out why.

Here's what's actually happening. Every professional carries the same set of blind spots into the same kinds of decisions. Your own expertise creates them. They're invisible to you by definition, which is why no amount of "working harder on the decision" helps. The only fix is to widen what's in the room when the decision gets made. That used to be prohibitive - you'd need a research team, a consulting engagement, or a PhD on retainer. Now it takes an afternoon and a compiled wiki.

Below are the ten blind spots I see most often in the founders, investors, and clinical leaders I work with. Each one quietly costs real money. Each one has a cheap fix.

## The Ten Blind Spots

### 1. The Adjacent-Industry Blindspot

**The pattern.** You size an opportunity using only comparables from inside your own category. The regulatory arc, growth curve, or failure mode from an adjacent category that maps exactly onto your bet never enters the room.

**What it costs.** A **founder** I worked with was 80% committed to a market entry bet when a cross-industry pull surfaced a regulatory pattern from an adjacent category that had killed three analogous businesses in the prior decade. Her own team missed it because none of them had worked in that adjacent space. Cost of the insight: an afternoon. Cost of ignoring it would have been 18 months of runway.

**The fix.** Ingest two or three "adjacent" categories into your knowledge base and ask the agent to surface every structural analogue on any non-trivial strategic decision. Not "search for me." *Integrate them into the wiki once, then synthesize forever.*

### 2. The Behavioural Science Gap

**The pattern.** You design experiences, flows, prices, or incentives without importing the research on how humans actually decide. Your expertise in your craft is real. Your expertise in cognition is zero, and you never close the gap.

**What it costs.** UX flows that violate progressive disclosure and lose 40% of users at step two. Pricing ladders that anchor wrong. Incentive structures that optimise for the opposite of the outcome you want. Every one of these is a silent tax on every conversion funnel you run.

**The fix.** Pull three to five foundational behavioural-economics sources into `raw/` and let the agent compile concept pages linked to your personas and conversion points. Every design and pricing decision now runs against the research automatically.

### 3. The Historical Analogue Hole

**The pattern.** Your strategic call gets debated against the two or three reference cases people in the room happen to remember. The other 47 analogues - the ones that would actually settle the question - never get consulted.

**What it costs.** An **investor** running diligence on a Series B used to rely on his network and the pitch deck. He started ingesting every comparable outcome from the last decade into his wiki. His win rate on qualified deals diverged from the fund's historical median within four quarters - not because he was smarter, but because the evidence in the room was dramatically wider.

**The fix.** Every category decision deserves a "historical analogue sweep" before commitment. Ingest ten prior comparable situations. Let the agent synthesize. Debate the synthesis, not the anecdotes.

### 4. The Customer-Voice Vacuum

**The pattern.** You make pricing, positioning, and roadmap decisions from the five customer calls you remember. The other 195 in your CRM are invisible to every meeting you run.

**What it costs.** Pricing set to the loudest complaint, not the real distribution. Roadmaps shaped by the most recent call, not the strongest signal. Positioning drafted against the last objection you got, not the pattern across your book.

**The fix.** Pipe every call transcript into the wiki. The agent compiles persona pages that cross-reference every conversation - quotes, objections, signals. The next product decision runs against all 200 calls automatically, not five.

### 5. The Contradictory-Perspective Fatal Flaw

**The pattern.** You cannot articulate the strongest argument against your own current plan. That means the plan is narrower than you think and you don't know where the crack is.

**What it costs.** A **founder** committed to a product thesis three quarters in a row without once running a serious steel-manned critique. The competitor she'd underestimated didn't die - it just ate the market she assumed she was winning. She knew the counter-case existed. She just hadn't loaded it into any of her decisions.

**The fix.** After every major call, ask the agent to steelman the opposite position using whatever's in your wiki plus a web search. Read the steel-man. Update the plan. This alone is worth the cost of the whole system.

### 6. The Cross-Specialty Silo

**The pattern.** In regulated and multi-disciplinary work, the specialist across the hall sees the case differently - and you aren't asking. Most teams cap their collective intelligence at the boundary of whichever function is running the meeting.

**What it costs.** A **clinical leader** I work with ran a case-review audit: patients with overlapping conditions systematically received treatment plans informed by one specialty's frame only. The outcome variance across similar cases was enormous. The fix didn't require smarter clinicians. It required a wiki that cross-linked every adjacent specialty's perspective to every case type.

**The fix.** Build the knowledge base as a team. Every function's concept pages live in the same wiki. Every major decision pulls across specialties automatically.

### 7. The Upstream/Downstream Shadow

**The pattern.** Architecture decisions made without ops context. Product decisions made without support context. Hiring decisions made without operational-load context. Every function sees its own step and misses what breaks on either side.

**What it costs.** A senior hire that works on paper but overwhelms the team's coordination capacity. A product feature that ships clean and generates 30% more support tickets. An architecture decision that's correct locally and catastrophic at the system level.

**The fix.** The wiki holds concept pages for every function that touches the decision. Ops decisions read product and customer context. Product decisions read ops and engineering context. Nobody makes a call from a single specialty's lens.

### 8. The Pricing-Psychology Gap

**The pattern.** You make pricing and packaging calls with internal data and competitor screenshots. The forty years of pricing theory that would have warned you never enter the room.

**What it costs.** Bad pricing calls in growth-stage SaaS regularly cost 15-25% of ARR expansion over a twelve-month horizon. On a $5M book that's $750K-$1.25M of foregone revenue from one keyhole decision. Most pricing is still run on a Tuesday afternoon with a spreadsheet and a hunch.

**The fix.** Ingest a few foundational pricing texts once. The agent compiles principles against your packaging. Every subsequent pricing decision runs against the research and your own customer data simultaneously. Cost of the fix: one afternoon of reading plus ingest. Return: the difference between a decent price and a great one, forever.

### 9. The Talent-Market Signal

**The pattern.** You set compensation, interview rubrics, and hiring bars using only internal context. The external signal - what the market is actually doing with comp, signals, and movement patterns - never shapes the decision.

**What it costs.** Losing offers you shouldn't lose because your comp band drifted out of market without anyone noticing. Over-paying for profiles the market has quietly discounted. Running an interview loop that optimises for signals the best candidates stopped exhibiting two years ago.

**The fix.** Ingest public comp surveys, relevant hiring literature, and a running feed of market signals into the wiki. The agent surfaces the delta between your internal assumptions and the external reality before each hiring cycle.

### 10. The Meta Blind Spot

**The pattern.** You don't know which disciplines you should be consulting. This is the one that breeds all the others. Until you audit your inputs, you can't see what you're missing.

**What it costs.** Everything above. Every blind spot one through nine is downstream of this one.

**The fix.** Once a quarter, ask the agent to audit your wiki for coverage gaps. "What disciplines are under-represented for the decisions we're making?" "Which concept pages are orphans?" "What did we ingest last quarter that we never referenced again?" The agent names the blind spots. You pick which one to close first.

## The Wide-Aperture Reflex

The real shift isn't consulting ten more sources on your next decision. It's building the reflex that a call you'd consult two disciplines for now gets ten. The wiki is the infrastructure that makes that reflex cheap. Your judgment is still the filter. But what your judgment filters just expanded by an order of magnitude.

The professionals pulling ahead aren't smarter. They've just stopped accepting the keyhole as the fixed aperture on their decisions. Everyone else is still making the same narrow call, slightly faster. One is climbing. The other is treading water while the climb accelerates around them.

---

## Ask Yourself

1. **Which of the ten above cost you money in the last quarter?** Be honest. Name the specific decision. That's where the wiki investment pays for itself first.

2. **What disciplines are entirely absent from your current decision inputs?** Not "under-represented." Absent. Those are the ones compounding silently against you.

3. **If a rival ran all ten of these checks on every major call and you ran none, who wins the year?** The answer is not subtle. The only question is how long you let it run.
